## Message 17 — Devpost copy + draft live demo narration script

> ## Inspiration
>
> Every company in the world is making decisions about humans using data that was never designed to understand humans. For example, let's say a nurse spends thirty minutes with a dying patient. The dashboard sees an overrun, and then the manager sends a message about cutting time. The action was right, but the system sees the decision as wrong.
>
> That's the gap we built amy to close. AI is good at handling the data. It's good at describing what people did, where they moved, and how long it took, but it can't tell you what they meant or why they did it. We watched footage across construction, healthcare, and education, all industries where a human gets reviewed by action data.
>
> ## What it does
>
> Amy is the AI that tells you how someone felt doing their job, so managers stop making decisions with just half the picture. Give it a video where a human is doing something and someone else will make a decision about it later. Amy predicts the second-by-second neural responses across about 20,000 cortical points using Meta's TRIBE V2 brain-encoding model, then writes a paragraph anchored to that pattern. Two numbers come with it: a similarity score against the brain pattern (how grounded the description is), and a falsification delta against control footage (to prove that it's anchored to *this* scene and not a generic LLM output).
>
> ## How we built it
>
> There are six stages.
> 1. Qwen3-VL describes what the dashboard would have seen without us, the action-data baseline.
> 2. Meta's TRIBE V2 predicts per-second neural response across ~20,000 cortical surface vertices on the fsaverage5 mesh, at 1Hz with a 5-second hemodynamic lag.
> 3. A K2 Think swarm on Cerebras (~2,000 tok/s, eight specialists in parallel, one per cortical region) interprets what each region was contributing.
> 4. Claude Opus 4.7 writes the paragraph.
> 5. 8 rounds where Claude writes a candidate and then TRIBE V2 scores it forward against the actual brain pattern, the score climbs round by round.
> 6. Last stage is a falsification check against a control video.
>
> The methodology comes from prior work. The iterative loop matches Clair de Lune's neural fingerprint to 90.4% last spring, run in reverse. The frontend is Vue 3 + Vite + Three.js (the cortical mesh renders all 20,000 vertices in sync with the footage at 30fps). The backend is FastAPI with a WebSocket layer.
>
> ## Challenges we ran into
>
> Honestly, the hardest part was just processing the videos. The TRIBE V2 pipeline started in Jupyter and the notebooks were *slow* because of the frame extraction, brain-encoding inference, writing per-second activity JSON for ~20,000 vertices, all of it. A 30-second clip felt like it took forever. We ripped it out of the notebook into a parallelized batch pipeline, with the frames extracted in one pass, TRIBE V2 inference batched on GPU, activity JSON serialized into a compact streamable format. A 30-second clip went from minutes to seconds. The output lives behind FastAPI as a pre-baked stream, with the live attempt running in parallel as a fallback.
>
> ## Accomplishments that we're proud of
>
> We inverted a published protocol. The Clair de Lune work had a 90.4% match across 20,484 vertices in the emotion center. It runs *forward* to score candidates against a target brain pattern. Amy runs the same loop in reverse, with the brain pattern as the source. We didn't invent the methodology this weekend, we used a credibility chip that was already public. 0.42 to 0.84 in 8 rounds. Under 60 seconds. Three cents a run. Falsification delta of 0.57 on the hero footage (0.84 vs 0.27).
>
> ## What we learned
>
> The line between *predicting neural response* and *reverse-inferring feelings* is the whole game. It's the difference between a product that augments management decisions and one that gets correctly torched by every neuroscientist in the room. Brain-grounding is what separates genuine cognitive description from confabulation. Any LLM can write *"she held space"* if you ask it, but the question is whether that paragraph would also score 0.84 against the actual brain pattern of the actual video. The score climbing is the falsifier. Without it, it's a style trick, but with it, it's a measurement.
>
> ## What's next for amy
>
> Workplace fine-tunes are must. TRIBE V2's canonical training set doesn't include workplace footage, and industry-specific fine-tunes. With fine tuning, we can close the gap on the inputs we actually want to serve.
>
> Next, the streaming inference since right now we run on 30-to-90-second clips. Eventually, we would like the architecture extends to streaming windows in real-time.
