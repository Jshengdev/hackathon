# The Idea Iceberg Framework

Adapted from game development validation principles for hackathon environments.

---

## The Concept

Imagine a sea of icebergs floating in an ocean. On the surface, they all look the same—any idea could be a winner or a flop. But below the surface shows something much different.

Your ability to see below the surface depends on your experience, skills, data, and validation methods.

---

## Level 1: Experience & Context

**What you can see:** The more you know about the space, the deeper you can evaluate ideas.

### Game Dev Application
- Genre knowledge
- Market awareness
- Player expectations

### Hackathon Translation
- **Track knowledge** — What does each sponsor actually want?
- **Judge profiles** — Who are they? What impresses them?
- **Past winners** — What patterns emerge?
- **Sponsor priorities** — What tech do they want showcased?

### How to Level Up
- Read ALL track descriptions carefully
- Research sponsors
- Look at past hackathon winners
- Ask mentors what judges look for
- Listen to what other teams are excited about

---

## Level 2: Skill & Feasibility

**What you can see:** An idea is only good if you can actually make it.

### Game Dev Application
- Team size constraints
- Technical capabilities
- Time to ship

### Hackathon Translation
- **Time constraint** — 5-6 hours? 24 hours? 48 hours?
- **Team capabilities** — Who can build what?
- **Hardware access** — What's available?
- **API access** — What can you integrate?

### The Filter Question
"Given our team, our time, and our resources—can we build a compelling demo of this?"

Not: "Can we build the full product?"
But: "Can we prove the concept with ONE working case?"

---

## Level 3: Market Research & Objectivity

**What you can see:** Data doesn't lie. First step into objectivity.

### Game Dev Application
- Steam sales data by genre
- Median earnings per category
- Top 1% benchmarks

### Hackathon Translation
- **Past winner analysis** — What actually won at this hackathon before?
- **Track competition** — How many teams typically target each track?
- **Judge preferences** — What do these specific judges reward?
- **Sponsor patterns** — What projects have sponsors highlighted before?

### Combat Bias
You WILL think your idea is brilliant. Everyone does. The data helps you see clearly:
- Is this track actually competitive?
- Are judges actually impressed by this type of project?
- Has this approach won before, or does it just feel clever?

---

## Level 4: External Validation (THE BIG ONE)

**What you can see:** Would people actually choose this?

### Game Dev Application
**The Question:** Would people BUY this game?

### Hackathon Translation
**The Question:** Would judges AWARD this project?

### The Gap

This is the critical insight:

| "Cool project" | "HOLY SHIT" |
|----------------|-------------|
| Polite nod | Judge leans forward |
| "Nice work" | "Tell me more" |
| Forgotten by finals | Remembered and mentioned |
| Maybe one track | Multiple track wins |

**The Gap** is the difference between these two reactions. Crossing The Gap is everything.

### Signals of Crossing The Gap

When testing your idea (pitch deck, explanation, early demo), look for:

1. **Physical leaning** — Do people lean in or sit back?
2. **Follow-up questions** — Do they ask more, or nod and move on?
3. **Retelling** — Do they explain your project to others?
4. **Effort increase** — Do playtesters/reviewers actively seek more?
5. **Memory** — Do they remember your project name later?

### The Work Back Method

Start from "we won overall + 3 tracks" and trace backwards:

| Stage | Evidence of Winning |
|-------|---------------------|
| Awards ceremony | Overall + multiple tracks |
| Final judging | Judges saying "this is different" |
| Demo | One case works, concept lands |
| Pitch | Clear story, memorable hook |
| Submission | Multi-track coverage |
| Build | 4 streams converged |
| Ideation | ← What would that idea look like? |

### Pitch Deck Validation

Before building, test your idea with a pitch deck:

1. Create 5-7 slides explaining the concept
2. Show to 3-5 people (ideally hackathon-experienced)
3. Watch their reactions (not just their words)
4. Look for Gap signals
5. Iterate or kill based on feedback

**Why this works:**
- Takes 30 minutes, not 3 hours
- Tests the IDEA, not the implementation
- Low sunk cost = easy to pivot
- Simulates first part of judge experience

## Level 5: Intuition

**What you can see:** The pattern recognition that only comes from experience.

### Game Dev Application
- "This feels like it will work"
- Micro-adjustments based on gut
- Knowing when to trust data vs. instinct

### Hackathon Translation
- Veteran sense for what wins
- Reading the room at opening ceremony
- Knowing when an idea "has it"
- Pattern matching to past successes

### How to Build Intuition
You can't shortcut this. It comes from:
- Doing more hackathons
- Watching what wins (and what doesn't)
- Reflecting on your own successes/failures
- Talking to consistent winners

### Using Others' Intuition
If you don't have it yet, borrow it:
- Find a mentor who's won before
- Ask them to gut-check your idea
- Watch their face, not just their words

## Applying the Framework

### Quick Validation Checklist

Before committing to an idea, run through:

**Level 1 (Context):**
- [ ] Do we understand this track deeply?
- [ ] Do we know what judges actually want?
- [ ] Have we researched past winners?

**Level 2 (Skill):**
- [ ] Can our team build a demo in time?
- [ ] Do we have the hardware/APIs we need?
- [ ] Is scope realistic?

**Level 3 (Objectivity):**
- [ ] What does the data say about this type of project?
- [ ] Are we being honest about competition?
- [ ] Have we checked our bias?

**Level 4 (Validation):**
- [ ] Have we tested the pitch with others?
- [ ] Did we see Gap signals (lean in, follow-ups)?
- [ ] Would judges remember this tomorrow?

**Level 5 (Intuition):**
- [ ] Does this feel right?
- [ ] What do experienced hackers think?
- [ ] Is there that spark of "this could actually win"?

---

## Common Failure Modes

### Stopping at Level 1
"I know this space well, so my idea must be good."
→ Experience without validation is just confident guessing.

### Skipping Level 4
"Let's just build it and see."
→ Wasted hours on ideas that don't cross The Gap.

### Over-indexing on Level 3
"The data says this track is less competitive."
→ Data without wow factor = optimizing for mediocrity.

### Ignoring Level 5
"The numbers say yes but it feels wrong."
→ Sometimes intuition catches what frameworks miss.

---

## The Meta-Insight

The Idea Iceberg isn't about finding the "objectively best" idea. It's about:

1. **Seeing more clearly** than others at the hackathon
2. **Validating early** so you don't waste time
3. **Crossing The Gap** between "cool" and "HOLY SHIT"
4. **Building confidence** that you're working on something worthwhile

Most hackathon teams operate at Level 1-2. Getting to Level 4 is a competitive advantage.
