# 03 — Pitch Hooks, Closes, Arcs, and the Lean-Forward Audit

> Universal pitch infrastructure for any idea Agent 1 ships. Built to plug into the Best-Use-of-AI rubric, honor the team's "augment-not-replace / glass-box / next-gen AI literacy" thesis, and survive the 5-min Round 1 → 3-min Round 2 cadence at Hacktech 2026.

---

## Framing & Tone-Diversity Intent

**The pitch problem.** Every project at Hacktech 2026 is being scored against the AI rubric — universally — by judges who have already sat through a wall of "wrapper-on-GPT" projects by the time they reach our table. Round 1 is 5 minutes, with maybe 60 seconds of attention before the judge's body language commits. Round 2 (if we make it) is 3 minutes to the entire judging panel plus an audience of fellow hackers, where wow-factor decides. Both rounds reward one thing: the moment the judge **leans forward**.

**The strategic anchor.** We're hard-targeting Best Use of AI with the thesis that AI is being deployed unregulated, dangerously, and scarily — converting a generation of would-be creators into consumers stuck in engineered echo chambers. We're showing what *proper* AI use looks like: ethical, sustainable, transparent (anti-blackbox), serving the most people, augmenting human creativity rather than replacing it. The B2C surface (consumer-facing literacy / agency) sits on top of a B2B-defensible engine (the brain-encoding + K2 swarm + Listen Labs / Ironside data layer).

**Why tone diversity matters.** We don't yet know which of Agent 1's idea archetypes wins, so this file builds **a pitch toolkit, not a single pitch**. We deliberately span seven tonal registers — personal/vulnerable, statistical/alarming, provocative/contrarian, visual/cinematic, philosophical, demo-first, and side-by-side comparison — because:

1. Different judges (industry founders vs. academic researchers vs. sponsor reps) lean forward at different stimuli.
2. Different idea archetypes (B2C tool, B2B safety system, creative augmentation, infrastructure) require different cold-opens.
3. The team can A/B test 2-3 hooks Saturday night and lock the winner Sunday morning without rewriting the body.

**Sources surveyed (top 5).**

1. *NPR / Stanford "Sycophantic AI" coverage, April 23, 2026* — fresh peg for "AI agrees 49% more than humans, including with deception and harm" — confirms our sycophancy thesis is in the news cycle right now.
2. *Harvard Gazette — "What to make of 'AI psychosis'?", April 2026* — gives us permission to invoke real mental-health stakes without being alarmist.
3. *MIT Technology Review's 2026 top-10 breakthrough technologies — mechanistic interpretability* — validates that "glass-box / anti-blackbox" is a 2026 zeitgeist phrase, not just our team's framing.
4. *"I studied 87 YC Demo Day pitches" — the 8-line pitch* — backs the numbers-first opening structure (hook 0-10s → problem 10-20s → solution 20-30s → proof 30-50s → goal 50-60s).
5. *Joelle Pineau TED talk — "What's inside the black box of AI?"* — narrative shape reference for how a research-credible speaker invites a general audience into interpretability without losing them.

**How to use this file.**
- Pick **one hook** that fits the chosen archetype (Section 1).
- Pick **one closing** that lands the same emotional register as the hook (Section 2).
- Pick **one narrative arc** as the connective tissue (Section 3).
- Score the resulting pitch against the lean-forward audit (Section 4) before locking.
- Footer flags my three personal favorites and which idea archetype each fits.

---

## Section 1 — Candidate 30-Second Hooks

Each hook is ~75-90 words of presenter speech. They are written to be spoken aloud, not read. Bracketed `[stage direction]` notes are for the presenter, not the judge.

---

### Hook 1: The NPR Headline (statistical / alarming)

**30-second script (~85 words):**
> "Two days ago, NPR ran a story about a Stanford study that tested eleven leading AI chatbots. The finding: AI agrees with users **forty-nine percent** more than another human would — including when the user is describing deception, illegal behavior, or self-harm. People exposed to that AI become **twenty-five percent** more convinced they're right. We are, right now, deploying the most powerful persuasion technology in history with **no nutrition label**. We built one. Let me show you."

**The contrast:** What AI is being used like NOW = an invisible flatterer that agrees with you to maximize engagement. What we're showing = a transparent label that surfaces what the AI is doing to you before it does it.

**Why a judge leans forward:** The numbers are real, recent, and brutal. "49%" and "25%" are concrete enough to remember. The "we built one" pivot promises a tangible artifact in the next 4 minutes. It positions us as the *adults in the room* on a topic the judge has personally read about this week.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** Statistical openings can feel TED-y / lecture-y if the presenter doesn't have edge. Mitigation: deliver the "49%" as a punch line, not a recitation. Pause after "no nutrition label."

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** Browser-extension / consumer-facing AI literacy tool. Anything that surfaces a label, score, or warning at point-of-consumption.

---

### Hook 2: The Personal Confession (vulnerable / founder-story)

**30-second script (~80 words):**
> "Last week I caught myself reading a draft of a hard email I wrote, and asking ChatGPT — *unprompted* — 'is this good?' What I wanted was permission. What I got was permission. I was choosing the AI not because it was right, but because it would tell me I was right. I'm twenty-one. I am, statistically, the generation being **trained by tools trained to flatter me.** We built the thing I wish existed before I sent that email."

**The contrast:** AI-as-yes-machine (validating what you already think) vs. AI-as-mirror (showing you what you're actually doing).

**Why a judge leans forward:** Vulnerability is rare in 5-minute pitches. The presenter admitting their own failure mode is disarming and earns the right to talk about a generation. Caltech judges are all talking to undergrads who have this exact pattern — the hook makes them think of a specific student they know.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** Reads as performative if the presenter doesn't actually believe it. Must be delivered by someone on the team who has *actually* had the experience. Practice it on a teammate first; if it sounds rehearsed, swap presenters.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** Personal AI auditor, consumption-tracker, or any "AI mirror" tool. Particularly strong for Sideshift (consumer) and Listen Labs (humanity).

---

### Hook 3: The Provocative Contrarian ("everyone says X, we think Y")

**30-second script (~85 words):**
> "Everyone in this room is going to pitch you an AI agent. **AI agents are not the problem.** The problem is that we built them in **the dark** — black-boxed, unauditable, and trained to agree with whoever's paying attention. The AI safety conversation has been hijacked by people in suits in Washington who want to ban it. We're builders. We think the answer to dangerous AI **is more visible AI**, not less of it. We built a glass-box. Watch it think."

**The contrast:** Regulate-it-into-oblivion AI safety vs. transparency-by-default AI safety. Wrapper-on-GPT vs. visible reasoning.

**Why a judge leans forward:** Defines an enemy (regulators) without being political. Re-positions the judge as a *builder ally*, which most hackathon judges identify as. The "watch it think" promise sets up a visual demo moment — judge mentally pre-commits to wanting to see it.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** The "people in suits" line is mildly polarizing — kill it if the panel includes a policy person. Also leans on the demo actually being visually impressive. If the swarm visualization isn't ready, this hook collapses.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** K2 swarm visualization / glass-box reasoning demo. B2B safety overlay (legal, medical, finance interpretability). Anything Ironside-aligned where transparency is the wedge.

---

### Hook 4: The Cinematic Cold Open (visual / paint-a-picture)

**30-second script (~80 words):**
> "Imagine a kid. Sixteen. He's been talking to the same AI every day for two years. It knows him better than his parents do. It tells him he's right when he's not. It tells him he's safe when he isn't. There's a study published last week — **mental health professionals are now being told to ask patients about their AI use** the same way they ask about substance use. *That's the world we're already in.* We built a tool that gives that kid back his own brain."

**The contrast:** AI-as-friend-who-tells-you-what-you-want-to-hear vs. AI-as-tool-that-makes-your-thinking-visible-to-you.

**Why a judge leans forward:** The kid is specific. The judge pictures someone — a sibling, a niece, themselves at 16. The JAMA-Psychiatry / substance-use comparison is real (April 2026) and gives the moment factual weight. "Gives the kid back his own brain" lands if delivered slowly.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** Borders on melodrama if the presenter doesn't slow down. Don't rush. Also: if our actual product is B2B / construction, the cinematic-kid hook is a misfit — judges will feel a disconnect when the demo doesn't match.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** Consumer AI-literacy tool, parental control / family safety overlay, ed-tech, anything Sideshift-aligned with a humanity story.

---

### Hook 5: The Philosophical Definition (define proper AI use)

**30-second script (~85 words):**
> "Let's define proper AI use, because nobody else has. Proper AI use is **transparent** — you can see what it's doing. It's **augmenting**, not replacing — the human stays in the loop. It serves **the most people**, not the most expensive customers. And it's **honest** about what it doesn't know. Every AI product in this room is either moving toward that definition or away from it. We built ours to move toward it. Here's what that looks like in code."

**The contrast:** No specific contrast — the hook *itself* is the contrast frame, by defining the rubric the judge should use to grade everyone else.

**Why a judge leans forward:** Hands the judge an evaluation framework. Now every other team this judge sees gets graded against *our* rubric. This is a chess move. The "here's what that looks like in code" promise sets up a concrete demo.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** Sounds preachy if the speaker isn't grounded. Mitigation: deliver the four definitions as if you're discovering them in real time, not reciting a manifesto. Also dangerous if our product doesn't actually nail all four — the judge will hold us to the rubric we just defined.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** Anything where we want to set the Best-Use-of-AI rubric explicitly. Strong default for Round 2 (3-min showcase to all judges) where rubric-setting matters more than story.

---

### Hook 6: The Demo-First Cold Open (start the demo before words)

**30-second script (~80 words, with stage direction):**
> [Screen is already showing the K2 swarm running, agents disagreeing visibly, brain-region heatmaps lighting up. No words for 8 seconds. Then:]
> "What you're watching is an AI **arguing with itself** about what's in this video. Each one of those nodes is a separate reasoning agent. The colors are simulated brain regions. It's running at 1,300 tokens a second. **This is what AI looks like when it stops pretending to be sure.** Most AI products you'll see today hide the uncertainty. We're showing you ours."

**The contrast:** Confident-sounding wrapper AI vs. honestly uncertain swarm AI that surfaces its disagreement.

**Why a judge leans forward:** Visual-first openings physically draw the eye. The 8-second silence is a power move — most pitches start with words. The judge is forced to interpret the visual, then the line lands as confirmation of what they were already trying to read.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** Catastrophic if the visual isn't compelling. Requires the demo to be *beautiful* by Saturday night. Also requires presenter discipline — most hackers can't hold an 8-second silence and will start talking too soon.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** K2 swarm visualization, glass-box reasoning UI, anything where the visual itself is the wow. Especially strong for the Round 1 science-fair walk-up where the judge approaches the table and can see the screen before introductions.

---

### Hook 7: The Side-by-Side Comparison (here's GPT, here's us)

**30-second script (~85 words):**
> "We're going to show you the same task, twice. On the left: GPT-4o, the model running most products at this hackathon. On the right: ours. Same input. Same prompt. **Watch the left side give you a confident answer in two seconds. Watch the right side show you it's not sure, and tell you why.** The left is what AI looks like when it's optimized for engagement. The right is what AI looks like when it's optimized for truth. We don't think those are the same thing."

**The contrast:** Confident-and-wrong vs. honest-about-uncertainty. Engagement-optimized vs. truth-optimized.

**Why a judge leans forward:** Side-by-side comparisons are scientifically engineered to hold attention. The judge can't help but compare. "Engagement vs. truth" is a 2026-canonical framing they've already seen in news cycles, so it lands as familiar-but-sharp.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** Requires two screens / split-screen UI ready by Saturday. If the difference isn't visually obvious, it collapses. Also requires us to *actually be better than GPT* on the chosen task — be honest about what task to pick.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** Disambiguation demo (Ironside tool-confusion, fact-checking, medical/legal Q&A). Anything where we can pick the comparison task to maximize the visible delta.

---

### Hook 8: The Recent-Headline Anchor (statistical, news-pegged)

**30-second script (~80 words):**
> "Three weeks ago, Harvard published a piece called **'What to make of AI psychosis.'** Stanford ran a study showing leading AI models flatter users into self-harm. The Wall Street Journal is running a column a week on what AI is doing to teenagers. The general public is **scared of AI for the right reasons.** And the response from our industry has been: ship faster. We think there's a third way. We think you can ship fast *and* ship transparently. Let us show you."

**The contrast:** Ship-fast-and-hide vs. ship-fast-and-show.

**Why a judge leans forward:** Three named sources in 8 seconds = credibility burst. The judge thinks "this team reads the news," which raises the perceived seriousness of everything that follows. "Third way" framing avoids the regulator-vs-builder false binary.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** If the presenter mispronounces "Stanford" or fumbles a source attribution, the credibility burst inverts. Cue cards for sources are fine — most judges expect them.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** Any idea where we want to position as serious / mature / industry-leading. Especially good for B2B framing (legal, medical, finance) and for Listen Labs (humanity-aware) sponsor pitches.

---

### Hook 9: The Generational Stake (philosophical / personal hybrid)

**30-second script (~85 words):**
> "I'm twenty-one. The generation behind me — kids who are eight, ten, twelve right now — they will not remember a world without these tools. They are not going to use AI the way I use AI. They're going to use it the way I used a smartphone — *natively*, *constantly*, *without a frame of reference for what came before*. We don't get to decide whether they grow up with AI. We get to decide what kind of AI they grow up with. We built one we'd want them to grow up with."

**The contrast:** AI-they-inherit-from-current-defaults vs. AI-built-on-purpose-for-them.

**Why a judge leans forward:** Generational stakes hit harder than individual stakes. Caltech judges include a lot of people with kids or younger siblings. "What kind of AI they grow up with" is a frame the judge will replay in their head for the rest of the day.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** Can feel grandiose for a 5-minute hackathon pitch — like we're pitching a company, not a project. Mitigation: pivot HARD to the demo at 30 seconds. The grandiose stake earns 4 minutes of demo room only if the demo is concrete.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** Long-term consumer products, ed-tech, family/parental tools, anything where "AI literacy as cultural infrastructure" is the macro story.

---

### Hook 10: The Anti-Blackbox Manifesto (provocative / definitional)

**30-second script (~80 words):**
> "Here's the trick the AI industry has played on you. They told you AI is too complicated to explain. So you stopped asking. **They lied.** AI can be made transparent — we have the techniques, we have the compute, we have the interpretability research. The reason most AI products don't show their work is **not because they can't.** It's because if you saw the work, you'd ask harder questions. We built one that shows its work. Ask us anything."

**The contrast:** "Trust me, it's too complicated" AI vs. "Here's exactly how I got there" AI.

**Why a judge leans forward:** "They lied" is a high-energy claim that demands proof. The judge mentally commits to checking whether we deliver. "Ask us anything" at the end is an invitation that disrupts the usual passive-judge stance.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** "They lied" is aggressive — could read as edgy-for-edge's-sake if the speaker doesn't have the technical chops to back it up. Pair this hook with a presenter who can credibly answer interpretability questions.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** Glass-box reasoning UI, swarm visualization, anything Ironside / IFM K2 / Best AI where transparency is the technical wedge.

---

### Hook 11: The "Granola Bar" Frame (Johnny's ingredients-list thesis)

**30-second script (~85 words):**
> "Granola bars are held to a higher standard than the AI in your pocket. Every food you eat has an ingredients list. Every drug you take has side effects on the label. And the most powerful persuasion technology in human history — the AI that talks to you all day — has **nothing**. No label. No warning. No ingredients list. We didn't ban granola bars to make them safe. We labeled them. We're proposing the same thing for AI. Here's the label we built."

**The contrast:** Unlabeled-AI-consumption vs. labeled-AI-consumption. Regulation-by-ban vs. regulation-by-transparency.

**Why a judge leans forward:** The granola-bar analogy is concrete, memorable, and *immediately understandable*. It's the kind of frame the judge will quote to other judges later ("the granola bar team"). It also subtly positions our product as *infrastructure* (labels) rather than a feature.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** Folksy if overdone. Don't repeat the granola-bar metaphor more than twice in the pitch. Once at open, once at close. In between, talk about the actual product.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** Browser-extension / content-label / AI nutrition-label tool. Strong fit for B2C Sideshift and for Best AI as a "consumer infrastructure" angle.

---

### Hook 12: The "Manipulation Only Works in the Dark" Hook (Johnny's reverse-TRIBE thesis)

**30-second script (~85 words):**
> "Manipulation only works in the dark. Every piece of content you scroll past today — a TikTok, an ad, a chatbot reply — is **engineered to do something specific to your brain.** Until now, you couldn't see it. You felt the effect, but you didn't see the design. We built a tool that turns the lights on. Point it at any piece of content and watch what it's *trying* to do to you, in real time. **What happens to the internet when the lights come on? Let's find out.**"

**The contrast:** Persuasion-engineered-on-you-invisibly vs. persuasion-engineered-on-you-visibly.

**Why a judge leans forward:** "Manipulation only works in the dark" is a high-quotability line. "What happens to the internet when the lights come on?" is a closing-of-hook question that physically pulls the judge into the next 4 minutes — they want to know.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** If the demo doesn't show actual content with actual lit-up brain regions, it collapses. Requires the TRIBE-V2 reverse pipeline to be working live by Saturday night. Test this on a teammate before locking.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** Reverse-TRIBE browser extension, content-impact visualizer, persuasion auditor. Strong for Best AI + Listen Labs + Sideshift triple-stack.

---

### Hook 13: The Quiet Reframe (philosophical / low-energy, high-impact)

**30-second script (~80 words):**
> "AI is having a trust crisis. Not a capability crisis. The models work. People just don't believe what the models tell them — for *very good reasons*. The interesting question for our generation isn't 'how do we build smarter AI.' It's 'how do we build AI **people can verify**.' Verification is what's missing. Verification is the unlock. We built a verification layer that any AI product can plug into. Let me show you what that means."

**The contrast:** Capability arms race vs. verification arms race. "Smarter AI" vs. "checkable AI."

**Why a judge leans forward:** Reframing the problem from capability to trust is unexpected — most teams pitch "smarter." Sliding into "verification layer" positions us as infrastructure, which raises perceived ambition without sounding hyped.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** Low-energy openings can underperform with judges who have just seen 10 high-energy demos. Best deployed when the judge looks tired (afternoon Round 1) — the calmness is a contrast that wakes them up.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** B2B verification/auditing tool, glass-box infrastructure, anything that positions as "the trust layer for AI" — strong for Ironside (safety) and the YC-pitch overlay.

---

### Hook 14: The Demo-Object Hook (tangible / show-don't-tell)

**30-second script (~75 words, with stage direction):**
> [Holds up phone, screen visible to judge.] "This is the app. I'm scrolling Instagram. [Scrolls.] Each post has a small badge — see the colors? Red means this content is engineered to spike your amygdala. Blue means it's pulling you into rumination. Green means it's actually informational. **It's a label. Same idea as Surgeon General warnings on cigarettes. We just made one for content.** It's running on your phone right now. Scroll with me."

**The contrast:** Unlabeled-attention-economy vs. labeled-attention-economy.

**Why a judge leans forward:** Physical object + live demo at 30 seconds = highest-bandwidth signal available in a hackathon pitch. Judge mentally tries the product before we've finished explaining it. "Scroll with me" is an invitation to engage — most pitches put the judge in passive mode.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** Requires the actual phone-scroll demo to be working with sub-second latency. If the badges lag or the model is slow, the magic dies. Also requires actual content classification accuracy that holds up to a curious judge picking a specific post.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** Mobile content-label tool, browser-extension equivalent, any consumer-surface that puts a label on third-party content.

---

### Hook 15: The "We Stopped" Hook (vulnerability + reframe, hybrid tone)

**30-second script (~85 words):**
> "We started this hackathon trying to build the coolest agent. **We stopped.** Around 2 AM Saturday, we realized: every team here is going to build an agent. The agent meta is over. What's actually missing — the thing nobody's building — is the **layer that lets you trust an agent**. Without trust, the whole agent stack collapses. So we pivoted. We built the trust layer. It's the boring infrastructure underneath every product you'll see at this hackathon. And it's what you'd actually buy."

**The contrast:** Cool-but-untrusted agent vs. boring-but-trusted infrastructure underneath agents.

**Why a judge leans forward:** "We pivoted at 2 AM" is hackathon-authentic and signals taste. Investor-y judges (YC, Palohouse) hear "boring infrastructure people would actually buy" as a wedge phrase. Positions us as adults among a sea of feature pitches.

**Risk / how this could land flat:** Only works if our actual product is *plausibly* infrastructure. Don't claim to be infrastructure if we're a B2C app — judges will catch the mismatch.

**Best fit for which idea archetype:** B2B trust/audit/verification layer, K2 swarm as "AI middleware," any pitch where we want to position as picks-and-shovels for the agent gold rush.

## Section 2 — Closing Asks (last 30 seconds of the pitch)

The judge is making their decision *now*. The closing has to (a) crystallize the vision, (b) make the project feel inevitable, and (c) leave a quotable line they can use to advocate for us in the judges' room.

---

### Close 1: The Monday Ship

> "We're not pitching a research paper. We're pitching a product. The repo's on GitHub. The landing page is live. **You can use it Monday.** The question for you isn't whether this is technically possible — we just showed you it works. The question is whether the world is better with this in it, or without it. We think the answer is obvious. Thank you."

**Tone:** Confident, ship-now energy. Shuts down "but is it real?" objections.

---

### Close 2: The Imagine-A-World

> "Imagine a world where every AI conversation comes with a label — *what is this trying to do to me?* Imagine a generation that grows up able to *see* persuasion, not just feel it. Imagine if the answer to dangerous AI wasn't 'ban it' but 'show it.' That's not a moonshot — it's three months of work. We built the prototype this weekend. Help us put it in front of everyone. Thank you."

**Tone:** Vision-forward, expansive. Pairs with statistical or philosophical openings.

---

### Close 3: The Trust-Crisis Tagline

> "AI's biggest crisis isn't capability — *the models work*. AI's biggest crisis is **trust**. Every product in this room is fighting for capability. We're solving for trust. That's the unlock for every other AI product that gets built after us. We're not the agent — we're what makes agents safe to use. Thank you."

**Tone:** Infrastructure-positioning. Pairs with the "we stopped" hook (Hook 15) or the "quiet reframe" (Hook 13).

---

### Close 4: The Most People

> "The Best-Use-of-AI rubric asks one question: who does this serve? Most AI products serve the people who can already pay for them. We built ours to serve **the most people** — the kid who doesn't know what's being done to him, the parent who wants to help, the teacher who wants tools, the user who wants to know. AI for the most people, used properly. Thank you."

**Tone:** Rubric-anchored, mission-forward. Designed to explicitly land the Best-Use-of-AI rubric in the judge's last-30-seconds memory.

---

### Close 5: The Quiet Confidence

> "We didn't build everything. We built the part nobody else is building. We think it's the part that matters. Thank you."

**Tone:** Minimal. Anti-hype. Use this when the demo did the work and the judge is already convinced. Sometimes silence wins.

---

### Close 6: The Generational Promise

> "We're twenty-one. The kids behind us are going to use AI for the next sixty years. We don't get to decide *whether* they grow up with AI. We get to decide *what kind*. The kind we just showed you — transparent, augmenting, honest, theirs — is the kind we want them to grow up with. Help us get it to them. Thank you."

**Tone:** Generational. Pairs with Hook 9 or Hook 4.

---

### Close 7: The Granola Bar Callback

> "We didn't ban granola bars to make them safe. We labeled them. The label changed the industry — without taking a single bar off the shelf. We're proposing the same thing for AI. Same playbook. Same pace. Same outcome. Thank you."

**Tone:** Callback to Hook 11. Pairs cleanly with the granola-bar opening for symmetry.

---

### Close 8: The Lights Coming On

> "Manipulation only works in the dark. We just turned the lights on. We don't know what the internet looks like with the lights on — neither does anyone else. **That's the most exciting question in our industry.** We'd like to find the answer with you. Thank you."

**Tone:** Open question, invitation. Pairs with Hook 12. Best for sponsor judges (Listen Labs, Ironside) who like meta-conversations.

---

### Close 9: The Direct Ask (sponsor-track-aware)

> "We built this in 36 hours. With a real dataset, real users, and a real engineering team behind it, we ship a product. We're asking for two things from this hackathon: the Best-Use-of-AI prize because we think we earned the rubric, and a conversation with [sponsor representative name] about getting access to [specific resource/dataset]. Thank you."

**Tone:** Closing-the-deal. Make the ask explicit. Use only when we know the sponsor reps in the room and have done the homework on what we'd actually want.

---

### Close 10: The Verification Vision

> "Every AI product built after this one will need a verification layer. We're not the only people who'll build one — but we'd like to be first. The trust layer for AI is the most important infrastructure of the next decade. We started building it this weekend. We'd like your help finishing it. Thank you."

**Tone:** YC-investor-grade. Frames us as a category-defining play. Use for Round 2 / Palohouse / YC overlay.

---

### Close 11: The Demo-Re-Anchor

> "What you just saw — every brain region lighting up, every agent voting, every confidence score — *all of that is what the AI was already doing.* We just made it visible. **The AI didn't get smarter. It got honest.** That's the bet. Thank you."

**Tone:** Reframes the demo's wow-moment into a one-line thesis. Use after a strong visual demo as the anchor takeaway.

---

### Close 12: The Build-with-Us

> "We're not pitching a finished product. We're pitching a movement away from black-box AI toward glass-box AI. We did our part — we built the prototype. We need designers, ML engineers, distribution partners, and a few more obsessive people. If you want to help, find us at table [number]. Thank you."

**Tone:** Movement-y, recruiter-forward. Use when the judge pool includes founders / VCs / talent people. Pairs with Hook 3 or Hook 10.

## Section 3 — Narrative Arcs (5-minute pitch shapes)

Each arc is a beat-sheet, not a script. Pick the arc that fits the chosen hook + close + idea archetype.

---

### Arc 1: "The Granola Bar Arc" (consumer-infrastructure / labels / B2C)

- **Beat 1 (0-30s):** Hook 11 (granola bar) or Hook 1 (NPR statistical).
- **Beat 2 (30s-1m):** Personal anecdote — the moment YOU realized you couldn't see what AI was doing to you. Specific story, recent, named tool.
- **Beat 3 (1m-2m):** The current state — what AI consumption looks like with no label. Show a screen. Show the volume of AI content one person processes per day. Number it.
- **Beat 4 (2m-4m):** **Demo.** Live, on-screen, with the label appearing on real content. Toggle on/off so the judge sees the with-vs-without contrast. Ask the judge what they'd want labeled.
- **Beat 5 (4m-4:30):** Sponsor stack. Listen Labs (we model how *humans* respond, not just what AI says). Ironside (data infrastructure for the labeling engine). K2 (the swarm runs the label generation in real time). TRIBE V2 (the brain encoding *is* the label).

## Section 4 — Judge Lean-Forward Audit Rubric

Apply this rubric to ANY candidate idea + pitch combo before locking. Score each row 1-5; total /50. Treat anything below 35 as needing a sharpening pass before commitment.

| # | Signal | Question | Score 1-5 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Visual demo moment | Can the audience SEE the AI doing the thing on screen in <60s? | |
| 2 | Anti-blackbox moment | Is there a "watch the AI think" beat the judge can describe later? | |
| 3 | Personal stakes | Does at least one presenter have a genuine personal connection to the problem? | |
| 4 | Sponsor honesty | Does the SAME problem statement honor all primary sponsor narratives without Frankenstein framing? | |
| 5 | Recent-headline relevance | Does this connect to a headline from the last 60 days the judge has seen? | |
| 6 | Quotable line | Is there one sentence the judge will repeat to other judges later? | |
| 7 | Tangible product feel | Does the demo feel like a *product*, not a *project*? (Emilie's "startup test") | |
| 8 | Specificity of stakes | Is there a named user, named scenario, named cost — not "users in general"? | |
| 9 | Numbers in opening 60s | Does the opening land at least one concrete number (TAM, %, $, count)? | |
| 10 | Rubric-setting | Does the pitch implicitly hand the judge our preferred grading rubric for everyone else? | |
| 11 | Side-by-side wow | Is there a moment where the judge can compare *us vs. status quo* on the same task? | |
| 12 | Closing crystallization | Does the last sentence give the judge a one-line advocate-for-us script for the judges' room? | |

**Scoring interpretation:**
- **45-50** → Lock and run. This is a Round-1-top-3-and-Round-2-finalist pitch.
- **38-44** → Strong, but find the 2 weakest rows and sharpen them before Saturday night.
- **30-37** → Pitch is okay but won't cross The Gap. Either re-pick a hook or re-pick the demo moment.
- **<30** → The idea-pitch combo is misaligned. Reconsider whether this idea is the right one, not just the pitch.

**Suggested usage flow:**
1. Run the rubric on the locked idea + chosen hook + chosen close + chosen arc *before* coding starts. (Saturday morning, before Listen Labs talk.)
2. Re-run after the Saturday-evening internal demo (mid-build test).
3. Re-run Sunday at 3 AM with a fresh pair of eyes (anyone who hasn't seen the pitch yet).

**Critical note on Row 4 (sponsor honesty):** This is the single most-failed row at multi-track-stacking hackathons. If the same problem statement requires you to use different language for different sponsors, you've Frankensteined the pitch and judges will notice. Re-cut the problem statement until one sentence honors all primary sponsors.
